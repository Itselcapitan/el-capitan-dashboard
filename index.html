▶ 📚 PRODUCTION GLOSSARY & WORKFLOW REFERENCE click to expand
🎶 Production Stages (in order)
1. Define Anchor
Before sound design, decide: what part of the original is the hook, what genre lane, what emotional job your version does better, what section should hit hardest. Goal: extract the identity and rebuild the energy.
2. Build 8-Bar Loop
Build order: kick → bass groove → clap/snare → hats/percs → main vocal chop or lead hook → one supporting synth/stab → one FX transition element. This is your remix DNA.
3. Vocal Prep
Warp/time-align vocal. Find key and BPM. Test full-speed, half-time, pitched, chopped sections. Create three lanes: clean lead vocal, chop lane, FX lane. Groove first, tone second.
4. Breakdown Sketch
Emotional chord/pad idea. Vocal feature moment. Build transition into the drop. This is the "breathing room" that makes the drop hit harder.
5. Full Arrangement
Copy 8-bar loop across timeline. Remove elements to create sections (subtractive method). Add transitions, builds, uplifters, variation. Don't over-produce the drop — pick one identity: vocal-led, bass-led, hook synth-led, or call-and-response.
6. Production Polish
Vocal throws, fills, drop variation, automation, ear-candy. Hats/percussion as energy automation — change hat length, stereo placement, transient feel, ride usage. Reverb as structure, not decoration.
7. Mix Prep
Individual channel cleanup. Gain staging. Remove unnecessary processing. Organize session: label tracks, color-code, group buses. Prep for focused mixing.
8. Mixing
Mix in layers: individual cleanup → kick/bass relationship → drum group → synth/music pocket → vocal pocket → FX support → mix bus polish. Keep mix bus simple. Reference constantly: original track + 2 pro targets + 1 structure reference.
9. Premaster Check
Leave headroom. Mono check. Transition check. Test on phone, headphones, car speakers. If it doesn't translate, go back to mix — don't fix it in mastering.
10. Mastering
Correction first, enhancement second. Loudness only after translation works. Check: true peak, LUFS, mono compatibility. Export final master + streaming versions.
11. Demo Out / Feedback
Send to feedback circle: DJ friends, producers, label contacts. Share bounce with specific questions ("how's the groove?", "does the drop land?"). Log all feedback with source, impact level, and action taken.
12. Revision Round
Process feedback. Make targeted adjustments. Don't re-open arrangement unless critical. Focus on mix tweaks, vocal levels, transition fixes. Re-bounce and re-check translations.
13. Teaser Ready
Track is good enough to post publicly. Create teaser assets: 15-30 sec drop clip, studio session clip, before/after comparison. This is your first public-facing content for the track.
14. Rollout Active
Full release campaign: teaser post → "should I drop this?" engagement post → release announcement → drop day post → follow-up/reaction post. Coordinate across IG, TikTok, SoundCloud. Run ads if budget allows. Tag original artists.
🎧 Translation Checks — What to Listen For
📱 Phone
Does the bass still have presence? Can you hear the vocal clearly? Does the kick cut through on tiny speakers? Low end often disappears — if the groove still works on phone, it'll work everywhere.
🎧 Headphones
Check stereo width, panning accuracy, vocal clarity, and detail in the high end. Listen for harsh frequencies, sibilance, and any artifacts that get lost on speakers but jump out in headphones.
🚗 Car
The real-world test. Does the sub hit right? Does the track feel exciting at volume? Are transitions smooth when driving? Car speakers exaggerate low-mid buildup — if it's muddy here, clean up 200-400Hz.
🔊 Speakers
Studio monitors or home speakers. Check balance across the full frequency range. Does the kick-bass relationship hold? Are synths sitting right in the mix? Is there enough headroom?
⚪ Mono
Collapse to mono and listen. Does anything disappear? Phase cancellation kills wide sounds in mono — club systems are often mono or near-mono. If the drop loses power in mono, fix stereo processing.
🎥 Content Assets — What Each One Is
Studio Clip
Screen recording or camera footage of you working on the track in your DAW. Shows the production process. Great for "making of" or "how I made this" content. Film during production, not after.
Teaser Clip
15-30 second clip of the best moment (usually the drop or vocal hook). This is your first public preview — the "should I drop this?" or "WIP" post. Hook the listener in 3 seconds.
Drop Clip
Isolated drop section (15-20 sec). The most shareable, replayable moment. Use for TikTok, Reels, and Stories. This is the clip that goes viral if the drop is good enough.
Crowd Clip
Video of a crowd reacting to your track live. The ultimate social proof. Film at gigs, house parties, car rides with friends. "Other DJs playing my track" is the top-performing format in the scene.
Reaction Clip
Someone hearing your track for the first time. Producer friend, DJ, random person. Genuine surprise/excitement is magnetic content. Film friends reacting at the studio or send the track and ask them to screen record.
Artwork
Release cover art for SoundCloud, streaming, and social posts. Can be simple (track title + your logo on a background) or elaborate. Needs to look good at 500x500 and as a tiny thumbnail.
Release Post
The main announcement post when the track goes live. Includes streaming link, artwork, caption with CTA ("link in bio", "COMMENT remix for the link"). This is the anchor post of your rollout.
Follow-Up Post
Second-wave content after release: thank you post, play count milestone, behind-the-scenes story, or repurposed teaser. Keeps the track in the algorithm after the initial push.
⚡ Full Workflow Summary
Phase A — Prep: Import original, set BPM/key, pull acapella/hook, choose 3 references.
Phase B — 8-Bar Drop Loop: Kick → bass → clap → hats/percs → hook vocal/chop → one lead/stab → basic FX.
Phase C — Breakdown Sketch: Emotional chord/pad idea, vocal feature moment, build transition.
Phase D — Full Arrangement: Copy loop across timeline, remove to create sections, add transitions/builds/uplifters.
Phase E — Production Polish: Vocal throws, fills, drop variation, automation, ear-candy.
Phase F — Mix: Individual cleanup → kick/bass lock → drum bus → music pocket → vocal pocket → FX balance → mix bus restraint.
Phase G — Master: Reference, loudness/true peak/mono checks, export.

Biggest rule: Finish fast, decide early, and only deepen processing after groove and arrangement already work.
